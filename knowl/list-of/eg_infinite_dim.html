Example 4.4.13. Infinite dimensional space.

Let \(\R^\infty\) be the vector space of infinite sequences of real numbers. (See ExampleÂ 1.1.11.) Prove that \(\dim \R^\infty=\infty\text{.}\)

Solution.
For each positive integer \(i\text{,}\) define \(\bolds_i\) to the infinite sequence whose \(i\)-th term is equal to one, and whose other terms are all equal to zero: i.e.,
\begin{equation*} \bolds_i=(0,\dots, 0,\underset{i\text{th term}}{1},0,\dots)\text{.} \end{equation*}
We will show that for all positive integers \(n\) the set
\begin{equation*} S_n=\{\bolds_1,\bolds_2,\dots, \bolds_n\} \end{equation*}
is linearly independent. It will then follow from CorollaryÂ 4.4.12 that \(\R^\infty\) is infinite dimensional, since it contains linearly independent sets of arbitrarily large cardinality.
ðŸ”—
To show \(S_n\) is linearly independent, assume we have
\begin{equation*} c_1\bolds_1+c_2\bolds_2+\cdots +c_n\bolds_n=\boldzero=(0,0,\dots) \end{equation*}
for some scalars \(c_1,c_2,\dots, c_n\in \R\text{.}\) Since
\begin{equation*} c_1\bolds_1+c_2\bolds_2+\cdots +c_n\bolds_n=(c_1,c_2,\dots, c_n,0,\dots)\text{,} \end{equation*}
we conclude that \(c_1=c_2=\cdots=c_n=0\text{,}\) proving that \(S_n\) is linearly independent.
