Example 2.1.7. Visualizing planes in \(\R^3\).

Let \(\mathcal{P}\subseteq \R^3\) be a plane defined by the nontrivial equation
\begin{equation} ax+by+cz=d\text{.}\tag{2.3} \end{equation}
Later we will describe an approach to visualizing \(\mathcal{P}\) in terms of its “normal vector” \(\boldn=(a,b,c)\text{.}\) For now we recall a more elementary approach. Namely, simply find by inspection three non-colinear points \(P,Q,R\) satisfying (2.3), sketch the triangle in \(\R^3\) they define, and then envision \(\mathcal{P}\) as the plane in \(\R^3\) containing this triangle. In practice we can find three such points by picking arbitrary values for two of the three variables, and solving for the third variable using (2.3).
🔗
For example, if \(\mathcal{P}\) is the plane with equation \(x+y+z=2\text{,}\) we see easily that \(P=(2,0,0), Q=(0,2,0), R=(0,0,2)\) lie on \(\mathcal{P}\text{,}\) allowing us to produce the graph in Figure 2.1.8.
🔗
As with lines, it is easy to see that the equation (2.3) defining \(\mathcal{P}\) is homogeneous if and only if \(\mathcal{P}\) passes through the origin \((0,0,0)\text{.}\)
