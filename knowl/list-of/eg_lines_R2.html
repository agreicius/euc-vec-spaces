Example 1.1.6. Visualizing lines in \(\R^2\).

Let \(\ell\subseteq \R^2\) be a line: i.e., \(\ell\) is the solution to some nontrivial linear equation \(ax+by=c\text{.}\) Since the equation is nontrivial, we have \(a\ne 0\) or \(b\ne 0\text{.}\) If \(b\ne 0\text{,}\) we may put the equation in the form
\begin{equation*} y=-\frac{a}{b}x+\frac{c}{b}\text{,} \end{equation*}
in which case we recognize \(m=-a/b\) as the slope of \(\ell\) and \(c/b\) as the \(y\)-intercept. If \(b=0\text{,}\) then \(a\ne 0\text{,}\) and the equation can be solved as \(x=c/a\text{,}\) which we recognize as the vertical line passing through the point \((c/a,0)\text{.}\)
It is easy to see that the equation defining \(\ell\) is homogenous if and only if \((0,0)\) is a point on \(\ell\text{:}\) i.e., \(\ell\) is defined by a homogeneous equation if and only if it passes through the origin.
