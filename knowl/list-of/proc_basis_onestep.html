Procedure 4.3.5. One-step technique for bases.

Let \(V\) be a vector space, and let \(B=\{\boldv_1,\boldv_2,\dots, \boldv_n\}\subseteq V\text{,}\) where the \(\boldv_i\) are distinct. To decide whether \(B\) is a basis, proceed as follows.
  1. Write out the vector equation
    \begin{equation} c_1\boldv_1+c_2\boldv_2+\cdots +c_n\boldv_n=\boldv\text{,}\tag{4.3.1} \end{equation}
    where \(\boldv\) is an arbitrary element of the vector space \(V\text{.}\) (Typically you will give some parametric description of \(\boldv\) in terms of a finite list of unknowns.)
  2. Translate the vector equation (4.3.1) into an equivalent linear system of equations in the unknowns \(c_1,c_2,\dots, c_n\text{.}\)
  3. If the system in (2) has a unique solution no matter what \(\boldv\) is chosen, then \(B\) is a basis. If there is a choice of \(\boldv\) for which either the system in (2) has no solution (i.e., is inconsistent) or has infinitely many solutions, then \(B\) is not a basis.
