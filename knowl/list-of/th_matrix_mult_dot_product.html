Theorem 3.1.25. Dot product and matrix multiplication.

Let \(m,n,r\) be positive integers. Given matrices \(A\in M_{mn}\) and \(B\in M_{nr}\text{,}\) let \(\bolda_i\in \R^n\) be the \(i\)-th row of \(A\) for all \(1\leq i\leq m\text{,}\) and let \(\boldb_j\in\R^n\) be \(j\)-th column of \(B\) for all \(1\leq j\leq r\text{,}\) so that
\begin{align*} A\amp=\begin{bmatrix}-\bolda_1-\\ -\bolda_2-\\ \vdots \\ -\bolda_m- \end{bmatrix} \amp B\amp= \begin{bmatrix} \vert \amp \vert \amp \amp \vert \\ \boldb_1\amp \boldb_2\amp \cdots \amp \boldc_r \\ \vert \amp \vert \amp \amp \vert \end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{align*}
For all \(1\leq i\leq m, 1\leq j\leq r\text{,}\) we have
\begin{equation} [AB]_{ij}=\bolda_i\cdot \boldb_j\text{.}\tag{3.1.3} \end{equation}
In other words, the \(ij\)-th entry of \(AB\) is the dot product of the \(i\)-th row of \(A\) and the \(j\)-th column of \(B\text{.}\)

Proof.

Let \(A=[a_{ij}]\) and \(B=[b_{ij}]\text{,}\) so that
\begin{align*} \bolda_i \amp = (a_{i1},a_{i2},\dots, a_{in})\\ \boldb_j \amp = (b_{1j},b_{2j},\dots, b_{nj}) \end{align*}
for all \(1\leq i\leq m\) and \(1\leq j\leq r\text{.}\) Given any pair \((i,j)\) with \(1\leq i\leq m\) and \(1\leq j\leq r\text{,}\) we have
\begin{align*} [AB]_{ij} \amp = \sum_{k=1}^na_{ik}b_{kj} \amp( \knowl{./knowl/xref/d_matrix_mult.html}{\text{3.1.21}}) \\ \amp =\bolda_i\cdot \boldb_j \amp (\knowl{./knowl/xref/d_dot_product.html}{\text{1.2.1}}) \text{,} \end{align*}
as claimed.
