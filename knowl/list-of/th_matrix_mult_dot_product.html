Theorem 3.1.21. Dot product and matrix multiplication.

Let \(m,n,r\) be positive integers. Given matrices \(A=[a_{ij}]\in M_{mn}\) and \(B=[b_{ij}]\in M_{nr}\text{,}\) define
\begin{align*} \boldr_i\amp =(a_{i1}, a_{i2},\dots, a_{in}) \amp \\ \boldc_j \amp = (b_{1j},b_{2j},\dots, b_{nj} \end{align*}
for all \(1\leq i\leq m\) and \(1\leq j\leq r\text{.}\) In other words, \(\boldr_i\) is the \(i\)-th row of \(A\text{,}\) considered as an \(n\)-tuple, and \(\boldc_j\) is the \(j\)-th column of \(B\text{,}\) considered as an \(n\)-tuple. For all \(1\leq i\leq m, 1\leq j\leq n\text{,}\) we have
\begin{equation} [AB]_{ij}=\boldr_i\cdot \boldc_j\text{.}\tag{3.1.5} \end{equation}
In other words, the \(ij\)-th entry of \(AB\) is the dot product of the \(i\)-th row of \(A\) and the \(j\)-th column of \(B\text{.}\)

Proof.

Fix a pair \((i,j)\) with \(1\leq i\leq m\) and \(1\leq j\leq r\text{.}\) We have
\begin{align*} [AB]_{ij} \amp = \sum_{k=1}^na_{ik}b_{kj} \amp( \knowl{./knowl/xref/d_matrix_mult.html}{\text{3.1.17}}) \\ \amp =\boldr_i\cdot \boldc_j \amp (\knowl{./knowl/xref/d_dot_product.html}{\text{1.2.1}}) \text{,} \end{align*}
as claimed.
