Example 1.1.8. Visualizing planes in \(\R^3\).

Let \(\mathcal{P}\subseteq \R^3\) be a plane defined by the nontrivial equation \(ax+by+cz=d\text{.}\) An elementary means of graphing \(\mathcal{P}\) in \(\R^3\) is to find three non-colinear points \(P,Q,R\) satisfying the equation, sketching the triangle in \(\R^3\) they define, and then envisioning \(\mathcal{P}\) as the plane in \(\R^3\) containing this triangle.
For example, if \(\mathcal{P}\) is the plane with equation \(x+y+z=2\text{,}\) we see easily that \(P=(2,0,0), Q=(0,2,0), R=(0,0,2)\) lie on \(\mathcal{P}\text{,}\) allowing us to produce the graph in FigureÂ 1.1.9.
