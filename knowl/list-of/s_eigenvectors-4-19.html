Example 6.1.23. Compute eigenspaces.

The characteristic polynomial of
\begin{equation*} A=\begin{amatrix}[rrrr] 3 \amp -1 \amp -1 \amp -1 \\ -5 \amp 2 \amp 2 \amp 3 \\ 6 \amp -1 \amp -2 \amp -3 \\ 1 \amp -1 \amp 0 \amp 0 \end{amatrix} \end{equation*}
is \(p(x)=t^{4} - 3 t^{3} + 3 t^{2} - 3 t + 2\text{.}\)
  1. Find all eigenvalues of \(A\text{.}\)
  2. For each eigenvalue \(\lambda\) compute a basis for the eigenspace \(W_\lambda\text{.}\)
Solution.
  1. To find the eigenvalues of \(A\) we must factor the polynomial \(p(t)\text{.}\) We first look for integer roots that divide the constant term \(2\text{.}\) We see that \(p(1)=0\text{,}\) and thus that \((t-1)\) is a factor of \(p(t)\text{.}\) Using polynomial long division, we see that
    \begin{equation*} p(t)=(t-1)(t^3 - 2t^2 + t - 2)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Continuing this process with the cubic polynomial, we factor
    \begin{equation*} t^3 - 2t^2 + t - 2=(t-2)(t^2+1)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Since \(t^2+1\) is irreducible over \(\R\text{,}\) we have our factorization
    \begin{equation*} p(t)=(t-1)(t-2)(t^2+1)\text{,} \end{equation*}
    and we see that the eigenvalues of \(A\) are \(\lambda=1\) and \(\lambda=2\text{.}\)
  2. Basis of \(W_1\).
    We have
    \begin{align*} W_1 \amp =\NS (I-A)\\ \amp = \NS \begin{amatrix}[rrrr] -2 \amp 1 \amp 1 \amp 1 \\ 5 \amp -1 \amp -2 \amp -3 \\ -6 \amp 1 \amp 3 \amp 3 \\ -1 \amp 1 \amp 0 \amp 1 \end{amatrix}\\ \amp = \NS \begin{amatrix}[rrrr] 1 \amp -\frac{1}{2} \amp -\frac{1}{2} \amp -\frac{1}{2} \\ 0 \amp 1 \amp \frac{1}{3} \amp -\frac{1}{3} \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 1 \amp -1 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \end{amatrix}\\ \amp =\Span\{(1,0,1,1)\}\text{.} \end{align*}
    We conclude that \(B=\{(1,0,1,1)\}\) is a basis of \(W_1\text{.}\)
    Basis of \(W_2\).
    We have
    \begin{align*} W_1 \amp =\NS (2-A)\\ \amp = \NS \begin{amatrix}[rrrr] -1 \amp 1 \amp 1 \amp 1 \\ 5 \amp 0 \amp -2 \amp -3 \\ -6 \amp 1 \amp 4 \amp 3 \\ -1 \amp 1 \amp 0 \amp 2 \end{amatrix}\\ \amp = \NS \begin{amatrix}[rrrr] 1 \amp -1 \amp -1 \amp -1 \\ 0 \amp 1 \amp \frac{3}{5} \amp \frac{2}{5} \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 1 \amp -1 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \end{amatrix}\\ \amp = \Span\{(1,-1,1,1)\}\text{.} \end{align*}
    We conclude that \(B'=\{(1,-1,1,1)\}\) is a basis of \(W_2\text{.}\)
